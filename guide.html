What This Calculator Does

This tool estimates an Agatston calcium score from a contrast-enhanced CTCA, so you don't need a separate non-contrast calcium scoring scan.

It uses a patient-specific HU threshold and a validated regression formula from the Nicol/Pavitt method.

1 Measure Contrast Attenuation

On your CTCA images, place a region of interest (ROI) in a contrast-filled vessel:

  • Where: Left main stem (LMS) or proximal right coronary artery (RCA)
  • ROI size: Approximately 0.1 cm²
  • What to record: The mean HU and standard deviation (SD) from the ROI
On your workstation: Use the elliptical ROI tool. Place it within the lumen, avoiding the vessel wall. Most CT workstations will display mean and SD automatically.
Tip: Choose a segment with good contrast opacification and minimal artefact. Avoid areas with stents, heavy calcification, or motion artefact.

Enter these two values (Mean HU and SD) into the first section of the calculator. The dynamic threshold will calculate automatically as you type.

2 Understand the Dynamic Threshold

In a standard non-contrast calcium score, the threshold is fixed at 130 HU. But on contrast CTCA, the vessel lumen is already enhanced well above 130 HU, so that threshold doesn't work.

Instead, this method calculates a patient-specific threshold:

Threshold = Mean HU + 2 × SD

This accounts for individual variation in contrast opacification. Any voxel above this threshold is considered calcification rather than contrast enhancement.

Why this matters: If contrast is very bright (high mean HU), the threshold rises accordingly, preventing contrast from being mistaken for calcium.

3 Score Calcification on CTCA

This is the key step. You are doing a calcium score on your contrast CTCA images, but instead of the standard 130 HU detection threshold, you use the dynamic threshold you just calculated (shown in the yellow box in Step 1).

What is the rCTCAS? It is simply the Agatston score your workstation produces when you run calcium scoring on the contrast CTCA dataset with the detection threshold changed from 130 HU to your calculated threshold. That is it — it is not a separate measurement or a different scoring system.

What you need to do:

  • Load your contrast CTCA axial dataset (not an MPR — the original axial slices, ideally reconstructed at 3 mm)
  • Open the calcium scoring module on your workstation
  • Change the HU detection threshold from 130 to your calculated threshold (e.g. 440 HU)
  • Run the automated scoring — the number it gives you is the rCTCAS
Why 3 mm? Standard Agatston scoring is validated on 3 mm axial slices. If your CTCA was reconstructed at thinner slices (e.g. 0.6 mm), you will need to create a 3 mm reformat or use your workstation's built-in slice thickness adjustment within the calcium scoring module.

3a On syngo.via (Siemens)

Step-by-step:

  • Open the CTCA dataset and launch the Ca Scoring application (or Calcium Scoring under CT Cardiac)
  • If the axial series is not automatically loaded, drag your contrast CTCA axial series into the application
  • In the scoring parameters panel, locate the Threshold field — this defaults to 130 HU
  • Change the threshold to your calculated value (e.g. 440 HU)
  • The software will highlight any voxels above that threshold in the axial images
  • Review and confirm the detected lesions (exclude any obvious artefact, e.g. stent blooming, contrast pooling in side branches)
  • The total Agatston score displayed is your rCTCAS — enter this into the calculator
Note: syngo.via may flag that contrast-enhanced data is being used. You can proceed — the modified threshold accounts for the contrast enhancement. If the software does not allow threshold adjustment, you may need to use the CT Cardiac Function or CT Vascular workflow as an alternative way to access the calcium scoring tools.

3b On TeraRecon (Intuition / Aquarius)

Step-by-step:

  • Load the CTCA dataset and open the Calcium Scoring module from the cardiac analysis tools
  • Ensure the axial source images are loaded (not thin-slice or MPR reformats)
  • In the scoring settings/preferences, locate the HU Threshold parameter — this defaults to 130 HU
  • Manually change this to your calculated threshold (e.g. 440 HU)
  • Run the auto-detect — the software will identify all regions above your threshold
  • Review each detected lesion: assign it to the correct vessel (LAD, LCx, RCA) if prompted, and exclude artefacts (e.g. contrast in a side branch, stent blooming, motion artefact at the RCA ostium)
  • The total Agatston score displayed is your rCTCAS — enter this into the calculator
Note: In some TeraRecon versions, the threshold adjustment is under Settings > Calcium Scoring > Detection Threshold or accessible via the gear icon within the scoring module. If the auto-detect picks up too much noise (especially at lower thresholds), increase the minimum lesion area to 1 mm² to filter out single-voxel noise.

What If My Workstation Won't Change the Threshold?

Some workstations lock the 130 HU threshold. In this case you have two options:

  • Option A: Use the workstation's ROI / measurement tools to manually identify and measure calcified lesions above your threshold on each axial slice. Measure the area (mm²) and peak HU of each lesion, then calculate the Agatston score manually using the standard weighting factors.
  • Option B: Export the DICOM data and use a research tool (e.g. 3D Slicer, ImageJ/FIJI with the Agatston plugin, or a custom Python script) that allows you to set an arbitrary HU threshold for calcium scoring.
Manual Agatston weighting reminder: For each lesion, multiply area (mm²) by a density factor: 1 if peak HU is 1–1.99× threshold, 2 if 2–2.99×, 3 if 3–3.99×, 4 if ≥4× threshold. Sum all lesion scores across all slices = rCTCAS.

4 Get Your Derived Score

Press "Calculate Derived CACS". The app applies the Nicol/Pavitt conversion formula:

CACS = (1.183 × rCTCAS) + (0.002 × rCTCAS × threshold)

You'll see:

  • Estimated Agatston Score — the derived calcium score
  • Risk category — from "No calcification" to "Severe" with a colour-coded bar
  • Working details — threshold used, raw score, and the full formula with your values
  • SCAD-specific reporting — simplified categorisation for SCAD cohorts (expandable)

Risk Categories

The calculator categorises results using standard Agatston thresholds:

  • 0 — No calcification detected
  • 1–10 — Minimal calcification
  • 11–100 — Mild calcification
  • 101–400 — Moderate calcification
  • >400 — Severe calcification
Key discrimination: The Nicol/Pavitt method shows strongest agreement with true CACS for distinguishing >400 vs <400 categories. Exact scores in the very low range (<10) should be interpreted with more caution.

Using This for SCAD Research

For SCAD cohorts, where calcium burden is typically expected to be near zero, the method has specific limitations:

  • Small thresholding errors from contrast, noise, or blooming can affect whether a lesion is counted at all
  • The method was validated across a broad range of scores, not optimised for near-zero precision

Recommended approach:

  • Primary categorisation: 0 vs >0
  • Secondary categorisation: 0, 1–10, >10 (only if image quality is excellent)
  • Frame as "derived contrast-CTCA calcium burden" rather than exact Agatston equivalent
When a low score is returned, the calculator will automatically display a SCAD-specific caveat and simplified categorisation to help you report appropriately.
