Created for clinical research and educational use

This calculator was developed to provide a practical, accessible tool for deriving coronary artery calcium scores from contrast-enhanced CT coronary angiography using the validated Nicol/Pavitt methodology. It is intended as an aid for clinicians and researchers working with CTCA data, particularly in settings where a dedicated non-contrast calcium scoring acquisition is not available.

Purpose

In standard practice, coronary artery calcium scoring requires a separate non-contrast CT acquisition. This adds radiation dose and scanning time. In many clinical and research scenarios — particularly retrospective datasets or streamlined CTCA-first pathways — only contrast-enhanced images are available.

This tool implements a validated method for estimating Agatston calcium score directly from contrast CTCA, enabling calcium risk stratification without needing a second scan.

The Nicol/Pavitt Method

The method was published by Pavitt, Harron, Lindsay, Nicol and colleagues, and proposes a three-step approach:

1. Patient-Specific Threshold

Rather than the fixed 130 HU threshold used in non-contrast calcium scoring, a dynamic threshold is calculated from the patient's own contrast attenuation:

Threshold = Mean HU + 2 × SD

This accounts for individual variation in contrast opacification and prevents enhanced blood from being misclassified as calcium.

2. Modified Agatston Scoring

Calcification is scored on axial 3 mm slices using the patient-specific threshold in place of the standard 130 HU, yielding a raw CTCA calcium score (rCTCAS).

3. Regression Conversion

The raw score is converted to an estimated Agatston equivalent using a validated regression equation:

CACS = (1.183 × rCTCAS) + (0.002 × rCTCAS × threshold)

Reported Performance

  • Excellent overall agreement with true (non-contrast) CACS
  • Sensitivity ~85%, specificity ~99% for risk stratification
  • Strong discrimination for >400 vs <400 Agatston categories
  • Potential to eliminate the need for a separate calcium scan, reducing radiation dose

Limitations

  • Contrast variability — differences in contrast timing, concentration, and injection rate can affect threshold stability
  • Blooming vs enhancement — overlapping attenuation profiles between dense calcification blooming and contrast enhancement
  • Low calcium scores — less accurate in the near-zero range, where small threshold errors can materially affect whether a lesion is counted
  • Not universally adopted — this method has not yet been incorporated into routine clinical reporting guidelines
SCAD cohorts: When calcium burden is expected to be 0 or minimal (as in SCAD), this method is at its weakest. Near-zero scores should be reported categorically (0 vs >0) rather than as precise Agatston equivalents.

Clinical Relevance

  • NICE pathways — where historically a calcium-first approach was used, this method can support CTCA-first strategies
  • Retrospective datasets — enables calcium scoring in cohorts where only CTCA exists (e.g. research databases, SCAD registries)
  • Radiation reduction — avoids dual acquisition (non-contrast + CTCA)
  • Research integration — can be combined with other CTCA-derived metrics such as FAI, tortuosity, and plaque characterisation

Reference

Pavitt CW, Harron K, Lindsay AC, Ray R, Sheppard MN, Wilde P, Defined E, Nicol ED.
